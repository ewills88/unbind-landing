How Much Time Do Divorce Attorneys Actually Spend on Admin?

The data shows family law attorneys spend 20+ hours per week on non-billable administrative work. Here's where the time goes—and how to get it back.

The Numbers Are Worse Than You Think

It is 6:45 PM on a Tuesday. You finished your last client meeting two hours ago. Since then, you have been sorting through a stack of discovery documents from a contested custody case, chasing down a retainer payment that is three weeks overdue, and responding to a client who has emailed four times today to ask whether her spouse has filed a response yet. You have not billed a single minute for any of it.

If this feels familiar, you are not alone. And the actual numbers behind how much time divorce attorneys lose to administrative work are far worse than most practitioners realize.

What the Data Actually Shows

The 2024 Clio Legal Trends Report found that the average attorney bills just 2.5 hours per day despite working 8 or more hours. That means roughly 70% of a lawyer's working time goes to tasks that never appear on a client invoice. For family law practitioners, the problem is even more acute. Divorce cases generate more documents, more communication, more scheduling conflicts, and more emotional client touchpoints than virtually any other area of practice.

2.5 hours/day
Average billable time per attorney—out of 8+ hours worked. The rest is consumed by admin, communication, and overhead.

We surveyed dozens of family law practitioners and analyzed time-tracking data from firms of all sizes. The picture that emerges is consistent: divorce attorneys spend between 16 and 24 hours per week on non-billable administrative tasks. Here is precisely where that time goes.

The Full Breakdown: Where 20+ Hours Actually Go

1. Document Management: 5–7 Hours/Week

A contested divorce can easily generate between 100 and 200 individual documents—financial disclosures, custody evaluations, interrogatories, motions, court orders, correspondence, and discovery materials. Each document needs to be received, reviewed, categorized, named according to firm conventions, and stored in the right case folder. At two to five minutes per document for manual sorting, the time adds up relentlessly.

And it is not just the initial filing. Documents get revised, superseded, and supplemented. Version control becomes its own job. Many attorneys report spending the equivalent of a full workday each week simply managing the paper trail of their active cases.

2. Client Communication: 4–6 Hours/Week

Divorce clients are, understandably, anxious. They are navigating one of the most stressful experiences of their lives, and they want to know what is happening with their case. The result is a steady stream of emails, phone calls, and portal messages—often five to ten status inquiries per client per month—that contain zero legal substance.

"Has my spouse responded yet?" "When is the next hearing?" "Did the judge sign the order?" These are legitimate questions, and they deserve answers. But composing individual replies to routine status inquiries across a caseload of 25 to 40 active matters consumes hours that could be spent on actual legal strategy.

3. Billing and Invoicing: 3–5 Hours/Week

Family law billing is uniquely painful. Divorce matters involve constant task-switching—a 12-minute call here, a 6-minute document review there, a quick email exchange that takes 0.2 hours. Capturing every fragment accurately requires discipline, and reconstructing time entries at the end of the day (or worse, the end of the week) leads to significant revenue leakage.

Then there is collections. Divorce clients are often navigating household income that has been cut in half. The average collection cycle in family law stretches to 45+ days, and managing follow-ups, payment plans, and trust account reconciliations adds yet another layer of administrative burden.

4. Scheduling and Deadline Management: 2–3 Hours/Week

Every divorce case carries a web of cascading deadlines—discovery cutoffs, motion filing windows, mandatory waiting periods, mediation scheduling requirements—and these deadlines vary by state, by county, and sometimes by judge. Missing a single deadline can be malpractice. Tracking them across 30 active cases using calendar entries and sticky notes is a recipe for both errors and anxiety.

Add in the logistical overhead of coordinating depositions, mediator availability, and opposing counsel schedules, and what should be simple calendar management becomes a significant weekly time sink.

5. Everything Else: 2–3 Hours/Week

The remaining hours disappear into a constellation of smaller tasks: updating case management software, preparing conflict checks, onboarding new clients, running reports for firm management, tracking CLE requirements, and dealing with IT issues. No single task takes long. Collectively, they consume two to three hours every week without fail.

Now consider the financial impact. At a billing rate of $400 per hour—which is moderate for experienced family law attorneys in most metro markets—20 lost billable hours per week represents $8,000 per week in unrealized revenue. Over 52 weeks, that figure reaches $416,000 per year. Even if you discount for realistic utilization targets, the gap between current performance and potential is staggering.

Why Generic Practice Management Tools Fall Short

If the problem is this well-documented, why hasn't existing technology solved it? Most family law firms have tried at least one practice management platform—Clio, MyCase, PracticePanther, or one of their competitors. These are competent general-purpose tools, and they do help with basic case organization and time tracking.

But they were not built for divorce. They do not understand that a dissolution case in California has fundamentally different deadlines than one in Texas. They do not know that a financial disclosure package requires specific forms by jurisdiction. They cannot automatically categorize an incoming document as a custody evaluation versus an asset appraisal. And they certainly cannot draft a routine status update to a client based on the actual current state of the case.

General practice management software treats a divorce case the same way it treats a personal injury claim or a contract dispute. The workflows, documents, and communication patterns are completely different—and that mismatch is where hours disappear.

The result is that attorneys end up doing the translation work themselves: manually configuring deadline rules, hand-sorting documents into categories the software does not recognize, and typing out the same status updates dozens of times per month. The tool helps at the margins, but it does not eliminate the core problem.

The Path Forward: Divorce-Specific Tools with AI

The practices that are reclaiming their time share a common approach: they use tools purpose-built for family law that leverage artificial intelligence to automate the tasks that consume the most hours.

Here is what that looks like in practice:

  • Automatic document classification that recognizes incoming filings, sorts them by type, and files them in the correct case folder without manual intervention.
  • AI-generated client status updates that pull real-time case data—filed documents, upcoming deadlines, pending tasks—and compose accurate, empathetic updates for attorney review.
  • State-specific deadline engines that automatically calculate filing windows, mandatory waiting periods, and discovery cutoffs based on jurisdiction and case type.
  • Intelligent time capture that logs billable activities as they happen, eliminating end-of-day reconstruction and the revenue leakage that comes with it.
  • Automated billing workflows that generate invoices, send payment reminders, and track trust account balances without manual intervention.

Firms using divorce-specific AI tools consistently report saving 15 or more hours per week on administrative tasks. That is not a theoretical projection—it is the difference between leaving the office at 5:30 and leaving at 8:00. It is the difference between 25 active cases feeling manageable and feeling impossible. And it is the difference between a practice that grows and one that plateaus because the attorney simply cannot take on more work.

The administrative burden in family law is real, it is measurable, and it is solvable. The only question is how long you are willing to keep losing 20 hours a week before you do something about it.
